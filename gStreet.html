My team and I delivered research-backed recommendations to city officials to help transform G Street into a more inclusive, multi-generational space.

Designed over eight weeks by a team of four designers, this case study walks through my end-to-end UX process, from conducting initial interviews and user research to synthesizing insights and developing actionable recommendations.

Strategic, Human-Centered Urban Design



Context

A local organization proposed rebuilding the downtown arch on G Street, now a pedestrian-only space. We needed to determine if the city should proceed or consider alternative uses.

Where is G Street?

Located at the heart of downtown Davis, surrounded by bars, restaurants, and retail. In 2021, the City closed G Street between Second and Third Streets to vehicle traffic as a temporary COVID-19 response to support local businesses. This pedestrian-only shift sparked discussion on how to improve the space.

Why the arch proposal?

The Davis Odd Fellows Lodge, established in 1870 as part of the global IOOF, proposed rebuilding the arch as a symbol of civic pride and unity. The original arch, however, only stood from 1916 to 1924, before being removed due to safety concerns as automobile traffic increased.

This history prompted an important question:
Would reinstating a symbolic landmark meaningfully address current community needs, or were there more impactful ways to activate the space?

Preliminary Research

We conducted six face-to-face interviews with three local Davis residents and three UC Davis students to understand how different groups perceived G Street and the proposed reintroduction of a historical landmark. Across both groups, participants expressed skepticism toward the arch proposal as a solution for activating the corridor, questioning whether a symbolic structure alone would meaningfully change how people use the space.

Rather than prioritizing historical novelty, both residents and students emphasized the need for the street to support everyday experiences, particularly opportunities to pause, gather, and engage with the environment in a more intentional way. These conversations highlighted a shared desire for functional, human-centered improvements over purely commemorative additions.

User Archetype: The Disconnected Student

This archetype represents students who regularly pass through downtown but rarely engage with G Street as a destination.

Values

They value experiences that feel wholesome and enjoyable, often preferring outings that provide meaningful connection without being overwhelming. Daytime activities are most appealing, as they align with their natural rhythms and provide a sense of safety and comfort. Above all, convenience and comfort play a major role in their decisions, with an emphasis on environments and activities that feel approachable and easy to access.

Fears

Their primary concerns center around wasting time on activities that don’t feel worthwhile or beneficial. They can feel hesitant about situations that involve too many new interpersonal interactions, which may feel draining or intimidating. Additionally, they worry about anything that might hinder their educational or social growth, as they want to ensure that their time is spent productively and in ways that align with their broader goals.


Mapping: Why‑How‑What & Empathy Mapping

Interviewees showed little interest in the arch proposal or lingering on G Street. To understand user motivations, frustrations, and barriers, we mapped responses using Why‑How‑What and empathy mapping techniques.

Key Findings:

❌ Users were generally uninterested in the arch as a destination.

❌ A pattern emerged: users only engaged with the street for specific tasks (shopping, passing through), then quickly left.

⚙️ How might we make G Street more active, inviting, and family-friendly, especially for non-drinkers?


Ideate

Based on these insights, we brainstormed several prototyping concepts to increase engagement and make the street welcoming to a broad audience:

  • Block parties and local vendor booths

  • Pop-up seating or performance spaces

  • Mini-golf and other interactive, all-ages activities

Mini-golf stood out because it:

  • Has all-ages appeal

  • Encourages social interaction without alcohol

  • Supports family and community friendly engagement


Why this matters:

The ideation process translated user research into concrete, testable concepts, ensuring that the solutions we prototyped addressed real barriers to lingering and participation identified in mapping.

Prototype Survey

Before prototyping, we conducted a survey to gauge whether users would find fulfillment in engaging with mini-golf on G Street. We received 9 responses, most of which indicated enthusiasm, describing it as a fun, convenient local outing. Several respondents even noted it as an appealing option for dates.

Prototype Testing

We conducted 11 on-site tests on G Street to observe public response directly. Feedback was overwhelmingly positive:

  • Mini-golf attracted attention from passersby

  • Encouraged friendly competition among friends and families

  • Provided a low-pressure, alcohol-free activity for the corridor

Concern

Safety risks for children, bikers, and skateboarders due to potential hazards from mini golf equipment and reduced street space.



Why this matters:

By testing directly in the street environment, we confirmed that activating the corridor with interactive, all-ages activities could change behavior and engagement patterns, supporting the broader design goal of making G Street a usable, inviting space.

Takeaways

While mini-golf was well-received and encouraged social interaction, survey and testing revealed the core issue was broader:

  • G Street lacked alcohol-free, inclusive social activities

  • Comfort, seating, and clear functional space were key for lingering

Key Insight:

The physical improvements made by the City, reconstructed sidewalks and additional seating, reflect and validate the user-centered findings of our research, showing that G Street’s activation depends on both engaging programming and purposeful public space design.

Key Achievements

Social and Environmental Impact

We developed a community-centered vision for downtown G Street, prioritizing safety, inclusivity, and sustainability. Our research emphasized what would encourage people to linger, interact, and feel comfortable in the space, particularly for non-drinkers and families.

Vision for the Future

Our team presented a comprehensive set of recommendations at City Hall to guide City Council in making long-term decisions that align with community needs and user behaviors. These recommendations went beyond symbolic improvements, focusing on functional, human-centered interventions such as seating, clear pedestrian flow, and family-friendly spaces.

Qualitative Outcomes

The project received positive feedback from residents and city council members, including formal recognition from the Davis City Council, underscoring the relevance and impact of our research. Community stakeholders noted that our work brought insights from everyday users into the city’s planning process.

Conclusions

Implementation Update

Following our recommendations, the City of Davis implemented substantial improvements to G Street:

  • Reconstructed sidewalks to improve pedestrian flow and safety

  • Expanded seating areas to encourage lingering and social interaction

Our team's work laid a strong foundation for more inclusive, community-centered design conversations, and demonstrated the potential of youth-led, research-driven advocacy. This experience underscored the importance of persistence in civic design and the value of community voices in shaping long-term change.

As a student researcher, I contributed across all project phases: interviews, synthesis, prototyping, and testing. While the project was highly collaborative, my prior experience in user testing allowed me to support and guide the team during fieldwork, encouraging team members to engage more confidently during interviews and prototype testing.
